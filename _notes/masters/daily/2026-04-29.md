```
  The all-pass filter is exactly magnitude-preserving in the continuous case, but the loss doesn't see the continuous case. random_phase_mangle (src/augmentations.py:32-40) is an IIR all-pass biquad applied via scipy.signal.lfilter. In theory it preserves the magnitude  
  spectrum exactly. In practice, what the loss sees is the STFT magnitude of a finite, windowed clip _ and that is not invariant under all-pass filtering:

  1. Boundary effects. lfilter runs from zero initial conditions over a fixed-length clip. The filter has a transient at the start (and effectively at the end, since the response extends past the clip but is truncated). Those boundary samples have different magnitude    
  content than the original. The STFT frames that overlap those boundaries see a real magnitude difference.
 
  2. Time-frequency localization. STFT magnitude is not the same as the full-signal magnitude spectrum. An all-pass filter introduces a frequency-dependent group delay _ energy at different frequencies gets shifted in time by different amounts. Within a single STFT      
  frame, that means energy that was concentrated in one frame can spread into adjacent frames (or vice versa), changing per-frame magnitudes even though the total magnitude spectrum across the whole clip is unchanged. Multi-scale STFT with the smallest windows is most sensitive to this.

  3. The decoder doesn't reproduce the all-pass distortion exactly. Even if magnitude is mostly preserved, the decoder is being asked to map an all-pass-filtered input back to the same all-pass-filtered target. The encoder/decoder don't have to learn the random          
  per-sample filter _ they can learn to produce some clean-ish reconstruction whose STFT magnitude approximately matches the augmented target's STFT magnitude. Across many random filters, the magnitude differences from (1) and (2) average out, so the network only has to match an "averaged" magnitude profile, not a specific one.

  So the actual mechanism is the opposite of what I wrote before: the all-pass augmentation does perturb STFT magnitudes (via boundary/windowing effects), but the perturbations are random and zero-mean. Across batches, the network can match the expected magnitude rather 
  than the per-sample magnitude _ and the loss is averaged over batches, so the residual variance becomes the floor. Validation has no such averaging benefit; every val sample must be matched exactly.
 
  There's also a simpler contributor I should have weighted more heavily: the _dequantize noise floor. Adding U[0, 1/65536] to the target raises the target's STFT magnitude floor in silent regions. The _l2_relative_distance (src/loss.py:66-67) divides by mean(y^2), so a 
  higher floor in y reduces the relative error wherever the network's output is close to zero. In quiet regions of percussive/speech audio this can be a meaningful effect. Validation has zero floor, so the relative L2 term is harsher in silent regions.

  Net answer: the asymmetry comes from (a) STFT-magnitude not being truly invariant under finite-clip all-pass filtering, but the perturbation being averageable across training samples, and (b) _dequantize's noise floor lowering the relative-L2 term on training targets.
```
